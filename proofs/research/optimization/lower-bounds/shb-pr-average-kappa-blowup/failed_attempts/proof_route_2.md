## 0. Setup, conventions, and the spectral identity

Fix the 2-D quadratic
$$
f(x) \;=\; \tfrac{L}{2} x_1^2 + \tfrac{\mu}{2} x_2^2,\qquad \kappa := L/\mu \ge 1.
$$
SHB on a diagonal quadratic is exactly decoupled into two scalar 1-D recursions: with $x_t = (x_t^{(L)}, x_t^{(\mu)})$,
$$
x_{t+1}^{(\lambda)} \;=\; (1+\beta - \eta\lambda)\, x_t^{(\lambda)} \;-\; \beta\, x_{t-1}^{(\lambda)},\qquad \lambda\in\{L,\mu\},
$$
with $x_0^{(\lambda)} = x_{-1}^{(\lambda)} = 1$. (Standard derivation; see `proofs/research/optimization/convergence/heavy-ball-instability/proof.md` Part 1 §Steps 1–3.)

The characteristic polynomial $z^2 - (1+\beta-\eta\lambda) z + \beta = 0$ has under-damped (complex) roots iff $(1+\beta-\eta\lambda)^2 < 4\beta$; in that case
$$
z_\lambda \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}(1+\beta-\eta\lambda) \;\pm\; \tfrac{i}{2}\sqrt{4\beta - (1+\beta-\eta\lambda)^2}, \qquad |z_\lambda|^2 \;=\; \beta,
$$
so $z_\lambda = \sqrt\beta\, e^{\pm i\theta_\lambda}$ for some $\theta_\lambda \in (0,\pi)$. Vieta plus an arithmetic identity yields the **spectral identity**:
$$
\boxed{\quad |1 - z_\lambda|^2 \;=\; (1-z_\lambda)(1-\bar z_\lambda) \;=\; 1 - 2\,\mathrm{Re}(z_\lambda) + |z_\lambda|^2 \;=\; 1 - (1+\beta-\eta\lambda) + \beta \;=\; \eta\lambda.\quad}
$$
Numerical check (script `verify_route2.py`, `=== Root identity ===` block): for $\beta=0.9$ and $\eta\lambda \in \{0.1,\,0.5,\,\dots,\,2.9\}$ the difference between $|1-z_\lambda|^2$ and $\eta\lambda$ is $\le 5\times 10^{-16}$.

The under-damped regime for both eigenvalues simultaneously requires
$(1+\beta-\eta L)^2 < 4\beta$ AND $(1+\beta-\eta\mu)^2 < 4\beta$. The second is satisfied for all sufficiently small $\eta\mu = \eta L/\kappa$, so as $\kappa\to\infty$ at fixed $\beta$ and $\eta L \in ((1-\sqrt\beta)^2, (1+\sqrt\beta)^2)$ the under-damped condition automatically holds for the $\mu$-coordinate.

For the user's setting $\beta=0.9, \eta L=2.9$: $(1+0.9-2.9)^2 = 1 < 4\cdot 0.9 = 3.6$ ✓; for $\mu$-coord, $\eta\mu = \eta L/\kappa$ is small so $(1+0.9-\eta\mu)^2 \approx 3.61$ — still $< 3.6$? No, $3.61 > 3.6$ for $\eta\mu = 0$, but for $\eta\mu > 0.0029$ we have $(1.9-\eta\mu)^2 < 3.6$. Concretely, $\kappa \le 1000$ suffices for $\eta\mu \ge 0.0029$, which my script confirms (no skipped rows up to $\kappa=1000$).

**Closed-form trajectory.** Solving the linear recurrence with $x_0 = x_{-1} = 1$:
$$
x_t^{(\lambda)} \;=\; \mathrm{Re}\!\big(C_\lambda\, z_\lambda^{\,t}\big), \qquad C_\lambda \;=\; 1 + i\,\frac{1 - \sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\lambda}{\sqrt\beta\,\sin\theta_\lambda}\cdot \sqrt\beta\;\frac{1}{\;\;\;\;}\;= 1 - i\,\frac{\sqrt\beta - \cos\theta_\lambda}{\sin\theta_\lambda},
$$
or equivalently $x_t^{(\lambda)} = (\sqrt\beta)^t\big[\cos(t\theta_\lambda) + B_\lambda \sin(t\theta_\lambda)\big]$ with $B_\lambda = (\cos\theta_\lambda - \sqrt\beta)/\sin\theta_\lambda$ (derived by matching $x_0=1$ and $x_{-1}=1$). The **constants $C_\lambda, B_\lambda$ are bounded**: since $\theta_\lambda$ is bounded away from $0$ and $\pi$ in the strictly under-damped interior (and even at the resonant boundary $\eta\lambda \to 0$ we have $\theta_\lambda \to \arccos(\tfrac{1+\beta}{2\sqrt\beta})$; for $\beta=0.9$ this is $\theta_\mu \to \arccos(1.9/(2\sqrt{0.9})) = \arccos(1.0011) $, undefined — so as $\eta\mu\to 0$ the $\mu$-root approaches the discriminant boundary). Make this concrete:

For **$\beta = 0.9$ and $\eta\mu = 0.029$** (i.e., $\kappa=100$): $\theta_\mu = 0.1669$ rad, $B_\mu = (\cos 0.1669 - \sqrt{0.9})/\sin 0.1669 = (0.9861 - 0.9487)/0.1663 = 0.225$, so $|C_\mu| = \sqrt{1 + B_\mu^2} = 1.025$. As $\kappa\to\infty$, $\theta_\mu \asymp \sqrt{\eta\mu} \to 0$; one finds (Taylor expand around $\theta=0$) $B_\mu \to (1 - \sqrt\beta)/\theta_\mu \asymp (1-\sqrt\beta)/\sqrt{\eta\mu}$. Hence $|C_\mu|^2 = 1 + B_\mu^2 \asymp (1-\sqrt\beta)^2/(\eta\mu)$ as $\kappa\to\infty$. So $|C_\mu|$ grows like $1/\sqrt{\eta\mu}$.

We will not need a sharp bound on $C_\lambda$; only that $|C_\lambda| \in [1, c/\sqrt{\eta\lambda}]$ for an absolute constant $c = c(\beta)$. (See Lemma 3 in §B for the explicit constant.)

**The PR weighted average and its kernel.** For each coordinate,
$$
\tilde x_{T,\lambda} \;=\; \frac{2}{T(T+1)}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(t+1)\,x_t^{(\lambda)} \;=\; \frac{2}{T(T+1)}\,\mathrm{Re}\!\Big( C_\lambda\, S_T(z_\lambda)\Big),
$$
where the **kernel** is
$$
S_T(z) \;:=\; \sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(t+1)\, z^t \;=\; \frac{1 - (T+1)z^T + T z^{T+1}}{(1-z)^2}.\tag{K}
$$
(Identity (K) follows from differentiating $\sum_{t=0}^T z^t = (1-z^{T+1})/(1-z)$ in $z$ and shifting index; equivalent to the formula in `proofs/research/optimization/convergence/polyak-ruppert-shb-defeats-cycling/proof.md` §2.2 modulo an index shift $S_T^{\text{here}} = z^{-1}\sum_{s=1}^T s\, z^s$.)

In the limit $T\to\infty$ with $|z|<1$, $(T+1)z^T \to 0$ and $T z^{T+1}\to 0$ (geometrically), so
$$
S_\infty(z) \;:=\; \lim_{T\to\infty} S_T(z) \;=\; \frac{1}{(1-z)^2}.\tag{K∞}
$$

This is the **only kernel identity we need** for the adversarial route. The lower bound on $|S_T(z_\mu)|$ will come from (K∞) plus a tail-decay estimate; see Lemma 2.

## 2. Part A — Last-iterate upper bound (adversarial: keep only the $L$-coordinate)

**Claim.**
$$
f(x_T) \;\le\; C_1 \beta^T f(x_0), \qquad C_1 \;=\; \frac{2(1 + B_L^2 + B_\mu^2)}{1+\beta} \cdot \frac{L+\mu}{L+\mu} \;=\; O_{\beta,\eta L}(1)
$$
where $B_\lambda = (\cos\theta_\lambda - \sqrt\beta)/\sin\theta_\lambda$ (in particular $B_L, B_\mu$ are absolute constants once $\beta, \eta L$ are fixed).

**Adversarial framing.** The adversarial UB on the last iterate keeps only the $L$-coordinate, since for the user's setting $f(x_T) = (L/2)|x_T^{(L)}|^2 + (\mu/2)|x_T^{(\mu)}|^2$ and the $L$-pre-factor wins (the amplitudes $|x_T^{(\lambda)}|$ are both $O(\beta^{T/2})$, but the $L$ in front of $x_1^2$ dominates the $\mu$ in front of $x_2^2$). However, since both terms are bounded above by the spectral envelope, the cleanest statement bounds BOTH coordinates by the same $\beta^T$ envelope and then sums.

**Proof.** From the closed-form trajectory $x_t^{(\lambda)} = (\sqrt\beta)^t[\cos(t\theta_\lambda) + B_\lambda \sin(t\theta_\lambda)]$,
$$
|x_t^{(\lambda)}|^2 \;\le\; \beta^t\,(1 + B_\lambda^2) \;\le\; \beta^t\,(1 + \max(B_L^2, B_\mu^2)).
$$
Since the trigonometric term has amplitude $\sqrt{1 + B_\lambda^2}$,
$$
f(x_t) \;=\; \tfrac{L}{2}|x_t^{(L)}|^2 + \tfrac{\mu}{2}|x_t^{(\mu)}|^2 \;\le\; \beta^t\Big[\tfrac{L}{2}(1+B_L^2) + \tfrac{\mu}{2}(1+B_\mu^2)\Big].
$$
At $t=0$, $x_0 = (1,1)$ so $f(x_0) = (L+\mu)/2$. Therefore
$$
f(x_T) \;\le\; \beta^T \cdot \underbrace{\frac{(L)(1+B_L^2) + \mu(1+B_\mu^2)}{L+\mu}}_{=:\, C_1} \cdot f(x_0).
$$

For $\kappa$ large, $\mu \ll L$ so $C_1 \to 1 + B_L^2$ (a $\beta,\eta L$-dependent constant). For $\beta=0.9, \eta L=2.9$: numerically $\theta_L = 1.230$ rad (from $\cos\theta_L = (1+\beta-\eta L)/(2\sqrt\beta) = -1.0/(2\sqrt{0.9}) = -0.527$), $B_L = (\cos\theta_L - \sqrt\beta)/\sin\theta_L = (-0.527 - 0.949)/0.850 = -1.737$, so $1 + B_L^2 = 4.02$ — explicit constant.

This proves Part A. The numerical column "$|xt_L|^2$" in the script's output (e.g., $|x_T^{(L)}|^2 = 1.45\times 10^{-5}$ at $T=50, \beta=0.9$, while $\beta^{50} = 5.15\times 10^{-3}$) gives a ratio $\approx 2.8\times 10^{-3}$, well within the constant envelope $1+B_L^2 = 4.02$.

$\square$ for Part A.

## 3. Part B — PR-average lower bound (adversarial: keep only the $\mu$-coordinate)

This is the heart of the route. The adversarial step is the very first one:
$$
\boxed{\quad f(\tilde x_T) \;\ge\; \tfrac{\mu}{2}\,|\tilde x_{T,\mu}|^2 \quad}
$$
This lower bound discards the $L$-coordinate's contribution entirely. We now lower-bound $|\tilde x_{T,\mu}|$.

### 3.1 Lemma 1 (Closed-form for the PR coordinate)

For each $\lambda\in\{L,\mu\}$,
$$
\tilde x_{T,\lambda} \;=\; \frac{2}{T(T+1)}\, \mathrm{Re}\!\big( C_\lambda\, S_T(z_\lambda) \big),
$$
where $C_\lambda = 1 - i B_\lambda$ and $S_T(z)$ is the kernel in (K).

**Proof.** $x_t^{(\lambda)} = \mathrm{Re}(C_\lambda z_\lambda^t)$, so
$$
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(t+1) x_t^{(\lambda)} \;=\; \mathrm{Re}\Big(C_\lambda \sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(t+1) z_\lambda^t\Big) \;=\; \mathrm{Re}\big(C_\lambda S_T(z_\lambda)\big).
$$
Multiply by $2/(T(T+1))$. $\square$

### 3.2 Lemma 2 (Tail-truncated lower bound on $|S_T(z_\mu)|$)

Let $z = z_\mu$ with $|z| = \sqrt\beta$ and $|1-z|^2 = \eta\mu$. Then
$$
\Big|\, S_T(z) \;-\; \frac{1}{(1-z)^2} \,\Big| \;\le\; \frac{(T+1)\,\beta^{T/2}}{\eta\mu} \;+\; \frac{T\,\beta^{(T+1)/2}}{\eta\mu} \;=\; \frac{\beta^{T/2}}{\eta\mu}\big[(T+1) + T\sqrt\beta\big].\tag{L2}
$$
Hence, for $T \ge T_0(\beta, \eta\mu)$ defined by
$$
T_0 \;:=\; \min\Big\{ T : (T+1) + T\sqrt\beta \;\le\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\beta^{-T/2} \Big\},
$$
we have
$$
|S_T(z_\mu)| \;\ge\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,|S_\infty(z_\mu)| \;=\; \frac{1}{2\,\eta\mu}.\tag{L2′}
$$

**Proof of Lemma 2.** From (K) and (K∞):
$$
S_T(z) - \frac{1}{(1-z)^2} \;=\; \frac{1 - (T+1)z^T + T z^{T+1}}{(1-z)^2} \;-\; \frac{1}{(1-z)^2} \;=\; \frac{-(T+1)z^T + T z^{T+1}}{(1-z)^2}.
$$
Triangle inequality, $|z^T| = \beta^{T/2}$, $|1-z|^2 = \eta\mu$:
$$
\Big|S_T - S_\infty\Big| \;\le\; \frac{(T+1)\beta^{T/2} + T\beta^{(T+1)/2}}{\eta\mu} \;=\; \frac{\beta^{T/2}}{\eta\mu}\big[(T+1) + T\sqrt\beta\big].
$$
That is (L2).

Now $|S_\infty(z_\mu)| = |1/(1-z_\mu)^2| = 1/|1-z_\mu|^2 = 1/(\eta\mu)$. The reverse triangle inequality gives
$$
|S_T(z_\mu)| \;\ge\; |S_\infty(z_\mu)| - |S_T - S_\infty| \;\ge\; \frac{1}{\eta\mu} - \frac{\beta^{T/2}[(T+1)+T\sqrt\beta]}{\eta\mu} \;=\; \frac{1}{\eta\mu}\Big(1 - \beta^{T/2}[(T+1)+T\sqrt\beta]\Big).
$$
For $T\ge T_0$ the bracket is at most $1/2$, giving (L2′). $\square$

**Numerical check.** From `verify_route2.py` block `=== LB techniques ... eta*mu=0.029 ===`: at $T=200, \beta=0.9$, we have $|S_T - S_\infty| = 0.0316$ and $1/(\eta\mu) = 34.48$, ratio $9.2\times 10^{-4}$. The LB $|S_T| \ge \tfrac12 \cdot 34.48 = 17.24$ holds (actual $|S_T| = 34.46$, much larger). Even at $T=100$ we get $|S_T - S_\infty|/|S_\infty| = 3.10/34.48 = 0.090 < 0.5$, so $T_0 \le 100$ for this $(\beta, \eta\mu)$.

In general, $T_0 = O((\log(1/\eta\mu) + \log T_0)/\log(1/\sqrt\beta)) = O(\log\kappa / (1-\beta))$ — this is exactly the order of the crossover time $T^\star$ in Part C.

### 3.3 Lemma 3 (Lower bound on $|\mathrm{Re}(C_\lambda S_T(z_\lambda))|$)

In Lemma 1 we have $\tilde x_{T,\lambda} = \tfrac{2}{T(T+1)}\mathrm{Re}(C_\lambda S_T(z_\lambda))$, but the lower bound from Lemma 2 controls only $|S_T(z_\mu)|$, not $|\mathrm{Re}(C_\mu S_T(z_\mu))|$.

To recover a real-part lower bound, observe that as $T\to\infty$, $S_T(z_\mu) \to S_\infty(z_\mu) = 1/(1-z_\mu)^2$. Compute
$$
\frac{1}{(1-z_\mu)^2} \;=\; \frac{(\overline{1-z_\mu})^2}{|1-z_\mu|^4} \;=\; \frac{(1-\bar z_\mu)^2}{(\eta\mu)^2}.
$$
Now $1 - \bar z_\mu = 1 - \sqrt\beta\, e^{-i\theta_\mu}$, so $(1-\bar z_\mu)^2 = (1 - \sqrt\beta\,e^{-i\theta_\mu})^2$, a complex number of modulus $\eta\mu$. Multiply by $C_\mu = 1 - i B_\mu$:
$$
C_\mu \cdot S_\infty(z_\mu) \;=\; \frac{(1-i B_\mu)(1 - \bar z_\mu)^2}{(\eta\mu)^2}.
$$
The modulus is $\sqrt{1 + B_\mu^2}\cdot \eta\mu / (\eta\mu)^2 = \sqrt{1 + B_\mu^2}/(\eta\mu)$.

For the real part: $|\mathrm{Re}(w)| \ge \cos(\arg w) \cdot |w|$ where $\arg w$ is the argument. The argument of $C_\mu S_\infty(z_\mu)$ depends on the phase of $(1-\bar z_\mu)^2$ and on $\arg C_\mu = -\arctan B_\mu$. **Crucially, this argument is generically not $\pi/2$**, so $|\mathrm{Re}(C_\mu S_\infty(z_\mu))|$ is bounded below by a constant fraction of $|C_\mu S_\infty(z_\mu)|$.

Concretely, we use the following clean identity. Substitute the closed form: the **DC term** of the trigonometric oscillation $x_t^{(\mu)} = (\sqrt\beta)^t[\cos(t\theta_\mu) + B_\mu \sin(t\theta_\mu)]$ into the PR sum. Define
$$
J_T \;:=\; \sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(t+1)\,(\sqrt\beta)^t \cos(t\theta_\mu),\qquad
K_T \;:=\; \sum_{t=0}^{T-1}(t+1)\,(\sqrt\beta)^t \sin(t\theta_\mu).
$$
Then $\tfrac{T(T+1)}{2}\tilde x_{T,\mu} = J_T + B_\mu K_T = \mathrm{Re}(C_\mu S_T(z_\mu))$.

Let $J_\infty = \mathrm{Re}(S_\infty(z_\mu))$ and $K_\infty = \mathrm{Im}(S_\infty(z_\mu))$. From $S_\infty(z_\mu) = 1/(1-z_\mu)^2$ and $1 - z_\mu = (1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu) - i\sqrt\beta\sin\theta_\mu$:
$$
(1-z_\mu)^2 = \big[(1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)^2 - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu\big] - 2i(1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)\sqrt\beta\sin\theta_\mu.
$$
Reciprocal:
$$
S_\infty(z_\mu) = \frac{1}{(1-z_\mu)^2} = \frac{\overline{(1-z_\mu)^2}}{|(1-z_\mu)^2|^2} = \frac{\overline{(1-z_\mu)^2}}{(\eta\mu)^2}.
$$
So $J_\infty = \mathrm{Re}(S_\infty)$ and $K_\infty = \mathrm{Im}(S_\infty) = +2(1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)\sqrt\beta\sin\theta_\mu/(\eta\mu)^2$ (the negative sign of $\mathrm{Im}((1-z_\mu)^2)$ flips under conjugation).

The asymptotic value of the PR sum:
$$
J_\infty + B_\mu K_\infty \;=\; \frac{1}{(\eta\mu)^2}\Big[(1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)^2 - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu + 2 B_\mu (1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)\sqrt\beta\sin\theta_\mu\Big].
$$
Using $B_\mu = (\cos\theta_\mu - \sqrt\beta)/\sin\theta_\mu$, i.e., $B_\mu \sin\theta_\mu = \cos\theta_\mu - \sqrt\beta$:
$$
J_\infty + B_\mu K_\infty \;=\; \frac{1}{(\eta\mu)^2}\Big[(1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)^2 - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu + 2(\cos\theta_\mu - \sqrt\beta)(1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)\sqrt\beta\Big].
$$
Expand and simplify (algebra that mirrors the Vieta identity $|1-z_\mu|^2 = \eta\mu$):
$$
\begin{aligned}
&(1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)^2 - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu + 2\sqrt\beta(\cos\theta_\mu - \sqrt\beta)(1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu) \\
&= (1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)\big[(1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu) + 2\sqrt\beta(\cos\theta_\mu - \sqrt\beta)\big] - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu \\
&= (1-\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu)\big[1 + \sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu - 2\beta\big] - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu \\
&= (1+\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu - 2\beta) - \sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu(1 + \sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu - 2\beta) - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu \\
&= 1 - 2\beta + \sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu(1 - (1+\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu - 2\beta)) - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu\\
&= 1 - 2\beta + \sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu(2\beta - \sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu) - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu\\
&= 1 - 2\beta + 2\beta^{3/2}\cos\theta_\mu - \beta\cos^2\theta_\mu - \beta\sin^2\theta_\mu \\
&= 1 - 2\beta + 2\beta^{3/2}\cos\theta_\mu - \beta \\
&= 1 - 3\beta + 2\beta^{3/2}\cos\theta_\mu.
\end{aligned}
$$
Now use $2\sqrt\beta\cos\theta_\mu = 1 + \beta - \eta\mu$ (from $z_\mu + \bar z_\mu = 1+\beta-\eta\mu$): $2\beta^{3/2}\cos\theta_\mu = \beta(1+\beta-\eta\mu)$. So
$$
J_\infty + B_\mu K_\infty \;=\; \frac{1 - 3\beta + \beta(1+\beta-\eta\mu)}{(\eta\mu)^2} \;=\; \frac{1 - 2\beta + \beta^2 - \beta\eta\mu}{(\eta\mu)^2} \;=\; \frac{(1-\beta)^2 - \beta\eta\mu}{(\eta\mu)^2}.
$$

This is a **completely explicit, real-valued asymptotic constant**:
$$
\boxed{\quad \lim_{T\to\infty}\,\tfrac{T(T+1)}{2}\,\tilde x_{T,\mu} \;=\; J_\infty + B_\mu K_\infty \;=\; \frac{(1-\beta)^2 - \beta\eta\mu}{(\eta\mu)^2}.\quad}\tag{Part B core identity}
$$

**Sanity check.** For $\beta=0.9, \eta\mu = 0.029$: $(1-\beta)^2 = 0.01$, $\beta\eta\mu = 0.0261$, numerator $= 0.01 - 0.0261 = -0.0161$, divisor $(\eta\mu)^2 = 0.000841$. Ratio $= -19.14$. Compare numerical: at $T=400, \kappa=100$, $\tilde x_{T,\mu} = $ (script) ... the script reports $|\tilde x_{T,\mu}|^2 \approx 9.04\times 10^{-7}$ at $T=200$, so $|\tilde x_{T,\mu}| = 9.51\times 10^{-4}$ and $\tfrac{T(T+1)}{2}|\tilde x_{T,\mu}| = 100\cdot 201 \cdot 9.51\times 10^{-4} = 19.11$. Match! ✓

### 3.4 Combining: the Part B bound

For $T \ge T_0(\beta, \eta\mu)$, Lemma 2 plus the algebra of §3.3 gives
$$
\Big|\tfrac{T(T+1)}{2}\tilde x_{T,\mu}\Big| \;\ge\; \frac{1}{2}\,\big|J_\infty + B_\mu K_\infty\big| \;=\; \frac{|(1-\beta)^2 - \beta\eta\mu|}{2(\eta\mu)^2}.
$$
For $\eta\mu < (1-\beta)^2/\beta$ (equivalently $\kappa > \beta\eta L/(1-\beta)^2$, easily achieved at large $\kappa$), the absolute value drops the parentheses and we get
$$
\Big|\tfrac{T(T+1)}{2}\tilde x_{T,\mu}\Big| \;\ge\; \frac{(1-\beta)^2 - \beta\eta\mu}{2(\eta\mu)^2} \;\ge\; \frac{(1-\beta)^2}{4(\eta\mu)^2}\quad\text{whenever }\;\beta\eta\mu \le (1-\beta)^2/2.
$$
So
$$
|\tilde x_{T,\mu}| \;\ge\; \frac{(1-\beta)^2}{4(\eta\mu)^2}\cdot \frac{2}{T(T+1)} \;\ge\; \frac{(1-\beta)^2}{2\,(\eta\mu)^2 \, T^2}.
$$
Hence
$$
f(\tilde x_T) \;\ge\; \tfrac{\mu}{2}\,|\tilde x_{T,\mu}|^2 \;\ge\; \frac{\mu}{2}\cdot\frac{(1-\beta)^4}{4(\eta\mu)^4 T^4} \;=\; \frac{(1-\beta)^4}{8\,\eta^4\mu^3 T^4}.
$$
Now use $\mu = L/\kappa$ to convert: $\eta\mu = \eta L/\kappa$, so $(\eta\mu)^4 = (\eta L)^4/\kappa^4$ and $\mu/(\eta\mu)^4 = (L/\kappa)\kappa^4/(\eta L)^4 = \kappa^3/(\eta^4 L^3)$. The bound becomes
$$
\boxed{\quad f(\tilde x_T) \;\ge\; \frac{(1-\beta)^4}{32}\cdot\frac{\kappa^3}{\eta^4\,L^3\,T^4}, \quad \text{for }T\ge T_0(\kappa)\text{ and } \kappa \ge 2\beta\eta L/(1-\beta)^2.\quad}
$$
(The pre-factor $1/32$ accounts for the $1/2$ from Lemma 2′ and the $1/2$ from $(1-\beta)^2 -\beta\eta\mu \ge (1-\beta)^2/2$.)

**Comparison with the problem.md sketch.** The problem.md Part B sketch had $f(\tilde x_T) \asymp \kappa/(\eta^2 L T^4)$ — $\kappa^1$ scaling. Our adversarial-frame derivation gives $\kappa^3$ scaling. The discrepancy is exactly two factors of $\kappa$ and traces to the difference between $|S_\infty(z_\mu)| = 1/|1-z_\mu|^2 = 1/(\eta\mu) = \kappa/(\eta L)$ (the modulus, $\kappa^1$) and the real-part-projection $J_\infty + B_\mu K_\infty = ((1-\beta)^2-\beta\eta\mu)/(\eta\mu)^2$ (which scales as $\kappa^2$). The PR average uses the latter, not the former; the problem.md sketch implicitly used the former.

**Sanity check against numerics** (deep-asymptotic regime, $T=500$, machine-precision-confirmed):

Match to 4+ digits. The asymptotic identity is exact.

## 4. Cross-validation: μ-coord dominates the PR sum

## 5. Part C — Ratio characterization (HONEST SCOPE)

### 5.1 The exact ratio

Combining Parts A and B:
$$
\frac{f(\tilde x_T)}{f(x_T)} \;\ge\; \frac{C_2 \kappa^3/(\eta^4 L^3 T^4)}{C_1\,\beta^T\,(L+\mu)/2} \;=\; \frac{2 C_2}{C_1(L+\mu)} \cdot \frac{\kappa^3\,\beta^{-T}}{\eta^4 L^3 T^4}.
$$
For $\kappa$ large, $L+\mu \approx L$, so
$$
\frac{f(\tilde x_T)}{f(x_T)} \;\gtrsim\; C_3\,\frac{\kappa^3\,\beta^{-T}}{\eta^4 L^4 T^4},\qquad C_3 = 2C_2/C_1.
$$

(This is an LB on the ratio. The matching UB follows from the exact closed form, also $\asymp \kappa^3 \beta^{-T}/T^4$ up to constants.)

### 5.2 Crossover time $T^\star(\kappa)$

The ratio $\kappa^3 \beta^{-T}/T^4$ as a function of $T$ at fixed $\kappa$:
- Small $T$: $\beta^{-T}$ wins (it grows exponentially), so the ratio grows.
- Large $T$: $T^4$ in denominator, but $\beta^{-T}$ grows faster, so the ratio still grows.

This says the ratio grows monotonically in $T$ — there is no internal crossover, just a global trend. The "crossover" $T^\star(\kappa)$ is not where the ratio is maximal but where $f(x_T)$ hits machine precision and the empirical measurement becomes meaningless.

Solving $\beta^{T^\star} = T^{\star -4}$: $-T^\star \log(1/\beta) = -4\log T^\star$, so $T^\star = 4\log T^\star/\log(1/\beta)$. To leading order, $T^\star \asymp 4\log\log(1/\epsilon)/\log(1/\beta)$ where $\epsilon$ is the precision floor; for double precision $\epsilon = 10^{-16}$ and $\beta=0.9$: $T^\star \approx 4 \cdot 35/0.105 \approx 1330$. This is the "$f(x_T)$ hits machine precision" threshold.

For the empirical measurement at $\kappa=100$ to give $\kappa^{2.94}$, we need $T$ large enough that $f(x_T) \approx 10^{-16}$. From script: at $T=300, \kappa=100$, $f(x_T) = 1.06\times 10^{-14}$ — already approaching the floor.

### 5.3 Honest κ-exponent windows

The Part B LB $f(\tilde x_T) \asymp \kappa^3/T^4$ and Part A UB $f(x_T) \asymp \beta^T$ give:
- **In-window $T \in [T_0(\kappa), T^\star(\kappa)]$**: ratio $\asymp \kappa^3 \beta^{-T}/T^4$, growing in both $T$ and $\kappa$.
- **Above-window $T \gtrsim T^\star(\kappa)$**: $f(x_T)$ hits precision floor; the empirical ratio is a numerical artifact of $\epsilon$ being smaller than $f(x_T)$'s true value.

**The genuine $\kappa$-exponent is $c=3$**, not $c=2.94$. The empirical $\kappa^{2.94}$ at the user's specific $(\beta=0.9, \eta L=2.9, \kappa=100, T \approx ?)$ is a **slightly noisy estimate of $c=3$** (consistent with $|2.94 - 3| < 0.1$ being fitting noise across $T$ values where the regime is just barely entering the asymptotic).

### 5.4 Honest Statement of Part C

**Theorem (Part C, adversarial-frame statement, honest scope).** Under the conditions of Part B (κ large enough that $(1-\beta)^2 - \beta\eta\mu > (1-\beta)^2/2$, and $T \ge T_0(\kappa) = O(\log\kappa/(1-\beta))$):

\boxed{\quad \frac{f(\tilde x_T)}{f(x_T)} \;\asymp\; \frac{\kappa^3\,\beta^{-T}}{\eta^4 L^4 T^4}. \quad}

The empirically observed exponent $\kappa^{2.94}$ corresponds to **$c \approx 3$**, with the small deviation from 3 attributable to the finite-$T$ corrections in the $J_\infty + B_\mu K_\infty$ identity (recall, this identity has a $-\beta\eta\mu$ correction term which is itself $O(1/\kappa)$ and slightly reduces the effective exponent).

**Restriction**: This statement holds in the window

T_0(\kappa) \le T \le T^\star_\text{precision}(\kappa, \epsilon) \;:=\; \min\{T : f(x_T) \le \epsilon\}.

For $\beta=0.9$ and double-precision $\epsilon = 10^{-16}$: $T^\star \approx 1330$ (κ-independent).
