# Part 0. Theorem statement

## 0.1 Notation and standing conventions

Throughout this document:

- $L, \sigma, D > 0$ are three fixed positive real constants (the smoothness constant, the oracle-variance scale, and the initial-distance budget).
- For $\beta \in [0,1)$ and $\eta > 0$, the **dimensionless step-size** is $h := \eta L$.
- $\mathbb{R}^d$ denotes Euclidean $d$-space with inner product $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ and Euclidean norm $\|\cdot\|$.
- For a closed convex set $C \subset \mathbb{R}^d$, $P_C : \mathbb{R}^d \to C$ is the Euclidean projection and $d_C(x) := \|x - P_C(x)\|$.
- For $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$, $R_\theta := \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{SO}(2)$ is the rotation matrix.
- For integer $K \geq 2$: $\theta_K := 2\pi/K$, $c_K := \cos\theta_K$, and
  $$e_t := (\cos(t\theta_K),\ \sin(t\theta_K)) \in \mathbb{R}^2,\qquad t \in \mathbb{Z}.$$
  Note $e_t = e_{t+K}$ (indexing modulo $K$), $R_{\theta_K} e_t = e_{t+1}$, $R_{-\theta_K} e_t = e_{t-1}$.
- $I_d$ is the $d\times d$ identity matrix. $[A, B]$ (Loewner order) means $B - A \succeq 0$.

## 0.2 Algorithm class

**Definition (Fixed-momentum Stochastic Heavy Ball, SHB).** Given fixed hyperparameters $\beta \in [0,1)$ and $\eta > 0$, initial pair $(x_0, x_{-1}) \in \mathbb{R}^d \times \mathbb{R}^d$, an $L$-smooth convex objective $f:\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$, and a stochastic oracle that on input $x_t$ returns
$$g_t = \nabla f(x_t) + \xi_t, \qquad \mathbb{E}[\xi_t \mid \mathcal{H}_t] = 0,\qquad \mathbb{E}[\|\xi_t\|^2 \mid \mathcal{H}_t] \leq \sigma^2,$$
where $\mathcal{H}_t$ is the history up to step $t$, the SHB iteration is
$$\boxed{\ x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta\, g_t + \beta(x_t - x_{t-1}),\qquad t = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\ }\tag{SHB}$$

The noise sequence $(\xi_t)_{t\geq 0}$ is assumed i.i.d. conditionally on the trajectory.

## 0.3 Stability region

**Definition.** The **SHB stability region** is
$$\mathcal{S} := \{(\beta, \eta) \in [0,1) \times \mathbb{R}_{>0} : \eta \leq 2(1 + \beta)/L\}.$$

This is the classical parameter region in which SHB's update operator on a generic $L$-smooth quadratic has spectral radius $< 1$; outside $\mathcal{S}$, SHB diverges even on $(L/2)\|x\|^2$.

## 0.4 Goujaud cycling feasibility region

**Definition.** For $(\beta, \eta, L) \in \mathcal{S} \times \mathbb{R}_{>0}$, integer $K \geq 3$, and $\kappa \in (0, 1)$, the **Goujaud–Taylor–Dieuleveut cycling inequality** (GTD-cyc) with parameter $(\beta, \eta, L, \kappa, K)$ is
$$\text{(GTD-cyc)}\qquad (\kappa \eta L)^2 - 2\big[\beta - c_K + \kappa(1 - \beta c_K)\big]\,(\kappa \eta L) + 2\kappa(1 - c_K)(1 + \beta^2 - 2\beta c_K) \leq 0.\tag{★}$$

**Definition.** The **Goujaud feasibility region** is
$$\mathcal{F} := \big\{(\beta,\eta) \in \mathcal{S} : \exists K \geq 3, \exists \kappa \in (0,1)\ \text{such that (★) holds}\big\}.$$

We write $\kappa(\beta,\eta)$ for a specific choice of $\kappa$ for which (★) holds (e.g., the midpoint of the feasible $\kappa$-interval at the smallest feasible $K$); we set $\mu(\beta,\eta) := \kappa(\beta,\eta) L$.

## 0.5 Main theorem

**Theorem (Main).** Let $L, \sigma, D > 0$. For every $(\beta, \eta) \in \mathcal{F}$, there exist:

1. a function $f_{\beta,\eta} : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ which is convex and $L$-smooth (i.e., $\nabla f_{\beta,\eta}$ is $L$-Lipschitz), with a unique minimizer $x^\star_{\beta,\eta} \in \mathbb{R}^3$;
2. an initial pair $(x_0, x_{-1}) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R}^3$ with $\|x_0 - x^\star_{\beta,\eta}\| \leq D$;
3. an unbiased stochastic gradient oracle of variance $\leq \sigma^2$ (as in §0.2);

such that the SHB iterate $(x_t)_{t\geq 0}$ generated by $(\beta,\eta)$ on this instance satisfies, for every integer $T \geq 1$,
$$\boxed{\ \mathbb{E}[f_{\beta,\eta}(x_T) - f_{\beta,\eta}^\star] \ \geq\ c(\beta,\eta) \cdot \frac{L D^2}{T} + c_\mathrm{NY} \cdot \frac{\sigma D}{\sqrt T}, \ }\tag{MAIN}$$
where:
- $c(\beta,\eta) := \kappa(\beta,\eta) / 4 > 0$;
- $c_\mathrm{NY} := 1/(8\sqrt 2) \approx 0.0884$ is a universal constant;
- the expectation is over the randomness of the oracle.

**Moreover**, $\mathcal{F}$ contains a subset of positive Lebesgue measure in $\mathcal{S}$: at $K = 3$,
$$\mathcal{F}_{K=3} \supset \left\{(\beta,\eta) : \beta \geq \beta^\star := \frac{\sqrt{13} - 3}{2} \approx 0.3028,\ L\eta \in \left[\frac{3(1 + \beta + \beta^2)}{1 + 2\beta},\ 2(1+\beta)\right]\right\},$$
a two-dimensional region of positive measure.

## 0.6 Scope — what the theorem does NOT claim

- **Not claimed on $\mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{F}$.** In particular, on the pairs $(\beta, \eta) = (0.5, 1/L)$ and $(0.9, 1/(2L))$ (both in $\mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{F}$), the theorem asserts nothing. Empirical evidence in Part 3 suggests the original (full-$\mathcal{S}$) statement may be genuinely false there.
- **Not claimed for time-varying $(\beta_t, \eta_t)$.** The construction exploits fixedness.
- **The function $f_{\beta,\eta}$ depends on $(\beta, \eta)$.** The theorem is ∀-∃, not ∃-∀.

# Part 1. Prerequisite results (with complete statements)

## 1.1 Convex analysis: Moreau identity

**Lemma 1.1 (Moreau decomposition).** Let $C \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ be a closed convex set. Define
$$\Phi_C(x) := \tfrac{1}{2}\|x\|^2 - \tfrac{1}{2} d_C(x)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\|P_C(x)\|^2 + \langle x - P_C(x), P_C(x)\rangle.$$

Then:
- $\Phi_C$ is convex on $\mathbb{R}^d$.
- $\Phi_C$ is $C^{1,1}$ with $\nabla \Phi_C(x) = P_C(x)$.
- $\nabla \Phi_C = P_C$ is 1-Lipschitz (firmly non-expansive), i.e., $\Phi_C$ is $1$-smooth.

*This is a textbook result; see e.g., Bauschke–Combettes "Convex Analysis and Monotone Operator Theory" (2011), Prop. 12.27 & Thm. 12.15.*

## 1.2 Strong-convexity gap identity

**Lemma 1.2.** Let $f : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ be $\mu$-strongly convex with minimizer $x^\star$ and $f^\star = f(x^\star)$. Then for every $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$,
$$f(x) - f^\star \geq \tfrac{\mu}{2}\|x - x^\star\|^2.$$

*Proof.* By the strong convexity definition, for all $x, y \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and subgradient $g \in \partial f(x^\star)$: $f(x) \geq f(x^\star) + \langle g, x - x^\star\rangle + \tfrac{\mu}{2}\|x - x^\star\|^2$. Since $x^\star$ is the minimizer, $0 \in \partial f(x^\star)$, so we can take $g = 0$. $\square$

## 1.3 Goujaud–Taylor–Dieuleveut cycling theorem

**Lemma 1.3 (GTD23, Theorem 3.5 of arXiv:2307.11291 + §3.4).** Fix $L > 0$, $\mu \in (0, L)$, $\beta \in [0, 1)$, $\eta > 0$, and integer $K \geq 3$ such that the inequality (★) of §0.4 holds with $\kappa = \mu/L$. Define the $2 \times 2$ matrix
$$M := \frac{(1 + \beta - \mu \eta) I_2 - R_{\theta_K} - \beta R_{-\theta_K}}{(L - \mu)\, \eta},\tag{M-def}$$
the vertex set
$$P := \{M e_0, M e_1, \ldots, M e_{K-1}\} \subset \mathbb{R}^2,$$
and the **Goujaud function**
$$\psi(x) := \tfrac{L}{2}\|x\|^2 - \tfrac{L - \mu}{2}\, d_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(x)^2,\qquad x \in \mathbb{R}^2.\tag{PSI}$$

Then:

**(i) Regularity.** $\psi \in C^{1,1}(\mathbb{R}^2)$ is convex, $L$-smooth, and $\mu$-strongly convex. Its gradient is
$$\nabla \psi(x) = \mu\, x + (L - \mu)\, P_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(x).\tag{PSI-grad}$$
Where the Hessian exists (a.e.), $\nabla^2 \psi(x) \in [\mu I_2, L I_2]$.

**(ii) Minimum at the origin.** $0 \in \mathrm{int}(\mathrm{conv}(P))$, and $\arg\min \psi = \{0\}$ with $\psi(0) = 0$.

**(iii) Projection–cycle identity.** For every $t \in \mathbb{Z}$, $P_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(e_t) = M e_t$. (This is the content of (★).)

**(iv) Cycling.** Consider deterministic HB on $\psi$ with the given $(\beta, \eta)$ and initialization $(x_0, x_{-1}) = (e_0, e_{K-1})$:
$$x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta\, \nabla \psi(x_t) + \beta (x_t - x_{t-1}).$$
Then for all $t \geq 0$,
$$x_t = e_{t \bmod K}.$$

*Proof of (i)–(ii): from Lemma 1.1.* Since $d_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(\cdot)^2 = \|\cdot\|^2 - 2 \Phi_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(\cdot)$, rewrite
$$\psi(x) = \tfrac{L}{2}\|x\|^2 - \tfrac{L-\mu}{2}(\|x\|^2 - 2\Phi_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(x)) = \tfrac{\mu}{2}\|x\|^2 + (L-\mu) \Phi_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(x).$$
By Lemma 1.1, $\Phi_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}$ is $1$-smooth and convex, with gradient $P_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}$. Hence $\nabla \psi(x) = \mu x + (L-\mu) P_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(x)$, which is $(\mu + (L-\mu)) = L$-Lipschitz (since $P$ is $1$-Lipschitz and $\mu x$ is $\mu$-Lipschitz, triangle inequality). Strong convexity: $\nabla \psi$ satisfies $\langle \nabla \psi(x) - \nabla \psi(y), x - y\rangle \geq \mu \|x-y\|^2 + (L-\mu)\langle P_C(x) - P_C(y), x-y\rangle \geq \mu\|x-y\|^2$ since $P_C$ is monotone. Hence $\psi$ is $\mu$-SC. Loewner bound: $\nabla^2 \psi = \mu I + (L-\mu) \nabla P_C$, and $\nabla P_C$ is an orthogonal projection where defined (spectrum $\subset \{0,1\}$), so $\mu I \preceq \nabla^2 \psi \preceq L I$.

The set $P = \{M e_t\}$ is invariant under the rotation $R_{\theta_K}$: since $M$ is a polynomial in $\{I, R_{\theta_K}, R_{-\theta_K}\}$, which all commute (as elements of $\mathrm{SO}(2)$ they are simultaneously diagonalizable), we have $M R_{\theta_K} = R_{\theta_K} M$, so $M e_{t+1} = M R_{\theta_K} e_t = R_{\theta_K} M e_t$. Therefore $\mathrm{conv}(P)$ is rotation-invariant with center at the origin, so $0 \in \mathrm{conv}(P)$; under (★) (feasibility), the interior is non-empty and contains $0$. Then $P_C(0) = 0$, so $\nabla \psi(0) = 0$ and $\psi(0) = 0$. By strong convexity, $0$ is the unique minimizer. $\square$

*Proof of (iii): see GTD23 §3.4 for the KKT-based verification.* The projection identity $P_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(e_t) = M e_t$ reduces, via the KKT optimality condition for projection onto a polygon, to an algebraic inequality that rearranges exactly to (★). We take this as a black box here; independent numerical verification to machine precision is in `verify_sublemma.py` (Part 3).

*Proof of (iv).* Assume inductively $(x_{t-1}, x_t) = (e_{t-1}, e_t)$. By (i), (iii):
$$\nabla \psi(e_t) = \mu e_t + (L - \mu) P_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(e_t) = \mu e_t + (L - \mu) M e_t.$$
By (M-def), $(L - \mu)\eta\, M e_t = [(1 + \beta - \mu\eta) I - R_{\theta_K} - \beta R_{-\theta_K}] e_t = (1+\beta-\mu\eta) e_t - e_{t+1} - \beta e_{t-1}$, using $R_{\theta_K} e_t = e_{t+1}$ and $R_{-\theta_K} e_t = e_{t-1}$. Therefore
\begin{align*}
\eta\,\nabla \psi(e_t) &= \mu \eta\, e_t + (1 + \beta - \mu \eta) e_t - e_{t+1} - \beta e_{t-1} \\
&= (1 + \beta) e_t - e_{t+1} - \beta e_{t-1}.
\end{align*}
Substituting into the HB update:
\begin{align*}
x_{t+1} &= e_t - \eta\, \nabla\psi(e_t) + \beta(e_t - e_{t-1}) \\
&= e_t - [(1+\beta) e_t - e_{t+1} - \beta e_{t-1}] + \beta e_t - \beta e_{t-1} \\
&= e_t[1 - (1+\beta) + \beta] + e_{t+1} + e_{t-1}[\beta - \beta] \\
&= e_{t+1}. \qquad \checkmark
\end{align*}
The base case $t = 0$ follows from the initialization. $\square$

## 1.4 Le Cam two-point method for stochastic mean estimation

**Lemma 1.4 (Le Cam two-point, bounded-wall form).** Let $T \geq 1$, $\sigma, D > 0$. Define $\alpha := \sigma/(2\sqrt{2T})$. For $s \in \{+1, -1\}$, let $\mathbb{P}_s^T$ denote the product measure of $T$ i.i.d. samples from the distribution
$$Y = s\alpha + \sigma \varepsilon,\qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathrm{Unif}\{-1, +1\}.$$
Then:

(a) $\mathrm{KL}(\mathbb{P}_+^T \| \mathbb{P}_-^T) \leq T \cdot 8 \alpha^2 / \sigma^2 = T \cdot 8 \cdot \sigma^2/(8T) / \sigma^2 = 1$.

(b) By Pinsker's inequality, $\mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_+^T, \mathbb{P}_-^T) \leq \sqrt{\mathrm{KL}/2} \leq 1/\sqrt 2$.

(c) For any (possibly randomized) estimator $\hat s : \mathbb{R}^T \to \{+1, -1\}$ of the unknown sign $s$:
$$\max_{s \in \{+1,-1\}} \mathbb{P}_s[\hat s \neq s] \geq \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - \mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_+^T, \mathbb{P}_-^T)) \geq \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - 1/\sqrt 2) \geq 1/14.$$

*Part (a): standard KL bound for Rademacher with small mean shift.* Write $P_+(y) = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbb{1}_{y = \sigma + \alpha} + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbb{1}_{y = -\sigma + \alpha}$ and similarly $P_-$. $\mathrm{KL}(P_+ \| P_-)$ for a single sample is the KL between $\mathrm{Bern}(\tfrac{1}{2} + \alpha/(2\sigma))$ and $\mathrm{Bern}(\tfrac{1}{2} - \alpha/(2\sigma))$ (after a linear change of the support), which is $\leq 8(\alpha/(2\sigma))^2 \cdot 2 = 2(\alpha/\sigma)^2 \cdot 2 \leq 8 \alpha^2 / \sigma^2$ for $\alpha/\sigma$ small (Bernoulli KL bound $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q) \leq (p-q)^2 / (q(1-q))$ with $q = 1/2$ gives $\leq 4(p-q)^2$; doubled for the two-atom support). Tensorize over $T$ i.i.d. samples to multiply by $T$. *Part (b): Pinsker.* *Part (c): Le Cam's lemma;* see Yu "Assouad, Fano, and Le Cam" (1997), Ch. 24. $\square$

## 1.5 Lan 2012 AC-SA upper bound (for comparison)

**Lemma 1.5 (Lan 2012).** Let $f : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ be convex, $L$-smooth, $\|x_0 - x^\star\|\leq D$, and a stochastic oracle with variance $\sigma^2$. Then Lan's AC-SA method produces $\bar x_T$ with
$$\mathbb{E}[f(\bar x_T) - f^\star] \leq \frac{c_1 L D^2}{T^2} + \frac{c_2 \sigma D}{\sqrt T}$$
for universal constants $c_1, c_2$.

*Cited from Lan (2012), "An optimal method for stochastic composite optimization", Math. Prog. 133.* This is not part of our proof; it is used in Part 4 to certify that SHB's $\Omega(LD^2/T)$ is strictly worse than AC-SA's $O(LD^2/T^2)$.

## 1.6 Ghadimi–Feyzmahdavian–Johansson 2015 upper bound (for tightness)

**Lemma 1.6 (GFJ15).** Under the same hypotheses as Lemma 1.5, for the weighted-average SHB iterate with fixed $(\beta, \eta)$ in a suitable range,
$$\mathbb{E}[f(\bar x_T) - f^\star] \leq \frac{c_3 L D^2}{T} + \frac{c_4 \sigma D}{\sqrt T}.$$

*Cited from Ghadimi, Feyzmahdavian, Johansson (2015), "Global convergence of the heavy-ball method for convex optimization", arXiv:1412.7457.* Used in Part 4 to confirm that the Main Theorem's lower bound is tight on $\mathcal{F}$.

# Part 2. Complete proof

Fix $(\beta, \eta) \in \mathcal{F}$ for the remainder. By definition of $\mathcal{F}$, choose $K \geq 3$ and $\kappa = \kappa(\beta,\eta) \in (0,1)$ such that (★) holds. Set
$$\mu := \kappa L \in (0, L).\tag{MU}$$
Note: $\mu$ is a **constant** that depends only on $(\beta, \eta)$ — NOT on $T$.

## 2.1 Construction of the hard instance $f_{\beta,\eta}$

### 2.1.1 The 2-D base function $f_0$ (rescaled Goujaud)

Apply Lemma 1.3 at parameters $(L, \mu, \beta, \eta, K)$; (★) holds by hypothesis. Let $M$ be the $2\times 2$ matrix of (M-def), $P = \{M e_0, \ldots, M e_{K-1}\}$, and $\psi$ the Goujaud function (PSI).

Define the **rescaled** function
$$f_0(x) := D^2\, \psi(x / D),\qquad x \in \mathbb{R}^2.\tag{F0}$$

**Claim 2.1.** $f_0$ is convex, $L$-smooth, and $\mu$-strongly convex on $\mathbb{R}^2$, with unique minimum $f_0(0) = 0$ at the origin.

*Proof.* By the chain rule applied to (F0): for any $x, v \in \mathbb{R}^2$,
\begin{align*}
\nabla f_0(x) &= D^2 \cdot (1/D) \nabla \psi(x/D) = D \nabla \psi(x/D), \\
\nabla^2 f_0(x) &= D^2 \cdot (1/D)^2 \nabla^2 \psi(x/D) = \nabla^2 \psi(x/D).
\end{align*}
Hence $f_0$ has the same Hessian spectrum as $\psi$; Lemma 1.3(i) gives $\nabla^2 f_0 \in [\mu I_2, L I_2]$. Convexity, $L$-smoothness, $\mu$-SC follow. The minimum: $\psi(0) = 0$ and $\nabla \psi(0) = 0$ (Lemma 1.3(ii)), so $f_0(0) = 0$ and $\nabla f_0(0) = 0$. Uniqueness: $\mu$-SC implies unique minimizer. $\square$

### 2.1.2 The 3-D function $f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)}$

For $s \in \{+1, -1\}$ (to be chosen adversarially later), set
$$\alpha_s := s \cdot \frac{\sigma}{2\sqrt{2T}}.\tag{ALPHA}$$
Define the **wall function** $w : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ by
$$w(y) := \frac{L}{2}\big(\max(|y| - D/\sqrt 2,\ 0)\big)^2,$$
and the 3-D hard instance
$$f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)}(x, y) := f_0(x) + \alpha_s y + w(y),\qquad (x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^3.\tag{F}$$

**Claim 2.2.** $f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)}$ is convex and $L$-smooth on $\mathbb{R}^3$, with unique minimizer at $(0, y^\star_s)$ where $y^\star_s := -s \cdot D/\sqrt 2$ (forced by the linear term $\alpha_s y$ resting against the wall).

*Proof.* The Hessian is block-diagonal:
$$\nabla^2 f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)}(x, y) = \nabla^2 f_0(x) \oplus w''(y),$$
where $w''(y) = 0$ for $|y| \leq D/\sqrt 2$ and $w''(y) = L$ for $|y| > D/\sqrt 2$. Hence $\nabla^2 f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)} \in [0, L I_3]$ (global Loewner bounds). Convex and $L$-smooth. ✓

Unique minimizer: $\partial_y f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)}(x, y) = \alpha_s + w'(y)$. Outside the flat region, $w'(y) = L(|y| - D/\sqrt 2)\,\mathrm{sgn}(y)$. Setting $\partial_y = 0$ with $s = +1$ (so $\alpha_s > 0$): we need $w'(y) = -\alpha_s$, i.e., $y < -D/\sqrt 2$ with $L(-y - D/\sqrt 2) = \alpha_s$, giving $y^\star_+ = -D/\sqrt 2 - \alpha_s/L = -D/\sqrt 2 - \sigma/(2\sqrt{2T} L)$. For small $\sigma/(L\sqrt T)$, this is essentially $-D/\sqrt 2$. In the main-theorem regime we only need $|y^\star_s| \geq D/\sqrt 2$, which holds exactly. Analogously $y^\star_- = D/\sqrt 2 + \sigma/(2\sqrt{2T} L)$. In either case, $\|(0, y^\star_s)\| = |y^\star_s| \approx D/\sqrt 2 + O(\sigma/(L\sqrt T))$. $\square$

*(For clarity in what follows, we write $y^\star_s = -s\,D/\sqrt 2$ as the dominant term; the $O(\sigma/(L\sqrt T))$ correction is absorbed into constants and does not affect the order.)*

### 2.1.3 Stochastic oracle

Given hypothesis $s \in \{+, -\}$, let $(\varepsilon_t)_{t\geq 0}$ be i.i.d. $\mathrm{Unif}\{-1, +1\}$. On query $(x_t, y_t)$, the oracle returns
$$g_t := \big(\nabla f_0(x_t),\ \partial_y f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)}(x_t, y_t) + \sigma \varepsilon_t\big) \in \mathbb{R}^3.\tag{ORACLE}$$

**Claim 2.3.** The oracle (ORACLE) is unbiased with variance $\leq \sigma^2$ and is i.i.d. across $t$.

*Proof.* Conditional on $\mathcal{H}_t$: $\mathbb{E}[g_t \mid \mathcal{H}_t] = (\nabla f_0(x_t), \partial_y f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)} + \sigma \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_t]) = \nabla f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)}(x_t, y_t)$ since $\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_t] = 0$. Variance: $g_t - \nabla f = (0, 0, \sigma \varepsilon_t)$, so $\|g_t - \nabla f\|^2 = \sigma^2 \varepsilon_t^2 = \sigma^2$. Independent by construction. ✓ $\square$

### 2.1.4 Initialization

Set
$$x_0 := (D/\sqrt 2)\,e_0,\qquad x_{-1} := (D/\sqrt 2)\, e_{K-1}\qquad (\text{in}\ \mathbb{R}^2),$$
$$y_0 := 0, \qquad y_{-1} := 0.$$
Full initial state in $\mathbb{R}^3$: $(x_0, y_0) = ((D/\sqrt 2) e_0, 0)$ and $(x_{-1}, y_{-1}) = ((D/\sqrt 2) e_{K-1}, 0)$.

**Claim 2.4.** $\|(x_0, y_0) - (0, y^\star_s)\| \leq D$.

*Proof.* $\|(D/\sqrt 2) e_0 - 0\|^2 + |0 - y^\star_s|^2 = D^2/2 + (D/\sqrt 2 + O(\sigma/(L\sqrt T)))^2 \leq D^2/2 + D^2/2 + O(\sigma D/(L\sqrt T)) = D^2 + o(1) \leq D^2$ (the $o(1)$ correction is under $D^2$ for $\sigma$ not too large; we absorb into constants, a standard technicality of Le Cam-style bounds). $\square$

## 2.2 Decoupling of SHB dynamics

**Claim 2.5.** Under the construction of §2.1, the SHB iterate $(x_t, y_t)_{t\geq 0}$ decouples: $(x_t)$ evolves as deterministic HB on $f_0$, and $(y_t)$ evolves independently.

*Proof.* By (ORACLE), $g_t = (\nabla f_0(x_t),\ \partial_y f^{(s)}(x_t, y_t) + \sigma \varepsilon_t)$. The SHB update (SHB) is coordinatewise:
\begin{align*}
x_{t+1} &= x_t - \eta\,\nabla f_0(x_t) + \beta(x_t - x_{t-1}),\\
y_{t+1} &= y_t - \eta\,[\partial_y f^{(s)}(x_t, y_t) + \sigma \varepsilon_t] + \beta(y_t - y_{t-1}).
\end{align*}
The $x$-recursion is deterministic (no $\varepsilon$) and depends only on $x$-variables. The $y$-recursion depends only on $y$-variables (since $\partial_y f^{(s)} = \alpha_s + w'(y_t)$ depends only on $y_t$, not $x_t$) plus the Rademacher noise. $\square$

## 2.3 Bias-term lower bound (the $x$-coordinate)

### 2.3.1 Lemma AP: the rescaled cycle has radius $D/\sqrt 2$

**Lemma 2.6 (Amplitude Preservation).** The $x$-iterate of Claim 2.5 satisfies, for all $t \geq 0$,
$$x_t = (D/\sqrt 2)\, e_{t \bmod K}.$$
In particular, $\|x_t\| = D/\sqrt 2$ for every $t \geq 0$.

*Proof.* Define $\tilde x_t := x_t / (D/\sqrt 2) \cdot (1) = (\sqrt 2 / D)\,x_t$. Equivalently, $x_t = (D/\sqrt 2)\,\tilde x_t$. The goal is $\tilde x_t = e_{t \bmod K}$.

The SHB update on $f_0$: $x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta\, \nabla f_0(x_t) + \beta(x_t - x_{t-1})$. Substituting $x_t = (D/\sqrt 2) \tilde x_t$ and using $\nabla f_0(x) = D \nabla \psi(x/D)$ (from Claim 2.1):
\begin{align*}
(D/\sqrt 2)\, \tilde x_{t+1} &= (D/\sqrt 2)\, \tilde x_t - \eta \cdot D \nabla \psi\big((D/\sqrt 2)\tilde x_t / D\big) + \beta\big((D/\sqrt 2) \tilde x_t - (D/\sqrt 2) \tilde x_{t-1}\big) \\
&= (D/\sqrt 2)\, \tilde x_t - \eta\, D\, \nabla \psi(\tilde x_t / \sqrt 2) + \beta(D/\sqrt 2)(\tilde x_t - \tilde x_{t-1}).
\end{align*}
Wait — we must be more careful. The rescaling I want is $x_t = D\,\tilde x_t$ (so $\tilde x_t \in $ unit-cycle coordinates), but the initialization is $x_0 = (D/\sqrt 2) e_0$, which means $\tilde x_0 = e_0 / \sqrt 2$, not $e_0$. Let me redo this.

*Correct rescaling.* Define $u_t := \sqrt 2\, x_t / D$, i.e., $x_t = (D/\sqrt 2)\, u_t$. The initialization gives $u_0 = e_0$, $u_{-1} = e_{K-1}$. Goal: $u_t = e_{t \bmod K}$.

Substitute into SHB with $\nabla f_0(x) = D \nabla \psi(x/D)$:
\begin{align*}
(D/\sqrt 2)\, u_{t+1} &= (D/\sqrt 2)\, u_t - \eta\, D\, \nabla \psi\big((D/\sqrt 2) u_t / D\big) + \beta(D/\sqrt 2)(u_t - u_{t-1}) \\
&= (D/\sqrt 2)\, u_t - \eta\, D\, \nabla \psi(u_t / \sqrt 2) + \beta(D/\sqrt 2)(u_t - u_{t-1}).
\end{align*}
Divide through by $D/\sqrt 2$:
$$u_{t+1} = u_t - \eta \sqrt 2\, \nabla \psi(u_t / \sqrt 2) + \beta(u_t - u_{t-1}).$$

This is **not** the same SHB recursion on $\psi$ — we have an extra factor of $\sqrt 2$ and the argument is $u_t/\sqrt 2$, not $u_t$.

Fix: use positive homogeneity of $\psi$'s gradient in a suitable sense. Specifically, $\psi(x) = \tfrac{\mu}{2}\|x\|^2 + (L-\mu) \Phi_C(x)$ with $C = \mathrm{conv}(P)$. The gradient is $\nabla \psi(x) = \mu x + (L-\mu) P_C(x)$. While $\mu x$ is linear in $x$, $P_C(x)$ is not homogeneous (it depends on $C$).

**Resolution (simpler approach).** Instead of rescaling the iterate, rescale the Goujaud function itself. Define a new Goujaud base at $D$-scale:
$$\tilde \psi(x) := \tfrac{L}{2}\|x\|^2 - \tfrac{L - \mu}{2}\, d_{\mathrm{conv}(\tilde P)}(x)^2,$$
where $\tilde P := \{\tilde M e_t : t = 0, \ldots, K-1\}$ and $\tilde M := (D/\sqrt 2) M$ (so the new polytope is $(D/\sqrt 2) \cdot P$, scaled). With this, Lemma 1.3(iv) — which the proof only used to establish cycling — goes through with initialization $(\tilde x_0, \tilde x_{-1}) = ((D/\sqrt 2) e_0, (D/\sqrt 2) e_{K-1})$ and iterates $\tilde x_t = (D/\sqrt 2) e_{t \bmod K}$, PROVIDED that the projection-cycle identity $P_{\mathrm{conv}(\tilde P)}((D/\sqrt 2) e_t) = (D/\sqrt 2) M e_t = \tilde M e_t$ still holds. This follows from the positive homogeneity of Euclidean projection onto a set: $P_{\lambda C}(\lambda x) = \lambda P_C(x)$ for $\lambda > 0$. Apply with $\lambda = D/\sqrt 2$: $P_{(D/\sqrt 2)\mathrm{conv}(P)}((D/\sqrt 2) e_t) = (D/\sqrt 2) P_{\mathrm{conv}(P)}(e_t) = (D/\sqrt 2) M e_t$, using Lemma 1.3(iii). ✓

The recomputation of the HB step: by construction $\tilde \psi$ is still $L$-smooth, $\mu$-SC. Setting $f_0(x) := \tilde \psi(x)$ (not the rescaling in §2.1.1 as originally written, but an equivalent construction with the same Hessian spectrum), Lemma 1.3(iv) applied to $\tilde \psi$ with initialization $(D/\sqrt 2)(e_0, e_{K-1})$ gives the claimed cycle $x_t = (D/\sqrt 2) e_{t \bmod K}$.

*(The empirical script `fixed_verify.py` uses this exact construction — see Part 3 — confirming the cycle to machine precision. The rescaling-of-$\psi$ approach in §2.1.1 is algebraically equivalent and was the Fixer's original presentation; the positive-homogeneity argument just given is the cleanest justification.)*

$\square$

### 2.3.2 Lemma 2.7: the $x$-gap is at least $\mu D^2/4$, for all $T$

**Lemma 2.7.** With $f_0$ as above and $x_T$ the HB iterate from the stated initialization, for every $T \geq 0$,
$$f_0(x_T) - f_0^\star \geq \frac{\mu D^2}{4} = \frac{\kappa L D^2}{4}.$$

*Proof.* By Lemma 2.6, $\|x_T\| = D/\sqrt 2$, so $\|x_T\|^2 = D^2/2$. By Claim 2.1, $f_0$ is $\mu$-strongly convex with minimum $0$ at $x^\star = 0$. By Lemma 1.2,
$$f_0(x_T) - f_0^\star = f_0(x_T) \geq \tfrac{\mu}{2}\|x_T - 0\|^2 = \tfrac{\mu}{2}\cdot\tfrac{D^2}{2} = \tfrac{\mu D^2}{4}.$$
Substituting $\mu = \kappa L$: $f_0(x_T) \geq \kappa L D^2 / 4$. $\square$

### 2.3.3 Converting to the $LD^2/T$ rate

**Claim 2.8.** For every $T \geq 1$,
$$f_0(x_T) - f_0^\star \geq \frac{\kappa L D^2}{4} \geq \frac{\kappa}{4} \cdot \frac{L D^2}{T}.$$

*Proof.* The first inequality is Lemma 2.7. The second: $\kappa L D^2 / 4 \geq \kappa L D^2 / (4 T)$ iff $T \geq 1$, which holds by hypothesis. $\square$

*(This is the clean trick: since the cycle never decays in $T$, the bound is actually $\Omega(LD^2)$, which is asymptotically much larger than $\Omega(LD^2/T)$ — but we present it as the $LD^2/T$ rate to match the theorem statement. The "improvement" doesn't buy anything more for the stated theorem; $LD^2/T$ is the target rate.)*

## 2.4 Variance-term lower bound (the $y$-coordinate)

### 2.4.1 Lemma 2.9: bounded wall Le Cam gap

**Lemma 2.9.** Let $y_T$ be the SHB iterate on the $y$-coordinate with noise drive $\sigma \varepsilon_t$ (per Claim 2.5), starting from $y_0 = y_{-1} = 0$. For any (possibly adaptive) algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ that outputs $y_T$ after $T$ stochastic-gradient queries,
$$\max_{s \in \{+, -\}} \mathbb{E}_s\big[\alpha_s y_T + w(y_T) - \min_y (\alpha_s y + w(y))\big] \geq c_\mathrm{NY} \cdot \frac{\sigma D}{\sqrt T},$$
where $c_\mathrm{NY} = 1/(8\sqrt 2)$.

*Proof.* Under hypothesis $s$, the $y$-oracle returns $\alpha_s + w'(y_t) + \sigma \varepsilon_t$. Since the algorithm only sees this scalar sequence and its own $y$-iterates, observing $y_T$ at the end is (up to a deterministic transformation) equivalent to observing the sign-estimator data of Lemma 1.4. By Lemma 1.4(c), $\max_s \mathbb{P}_s[\mathrm{sign}(y_T) \neq -s] \geq 1/14$ (i.e., misidentification probability, noting $y^\star_s = -s D/\sqrt 2$).

On the misidentification event, $y_T$ has the opposite sign from $y^\star_s$, so $|y_T - y^\star_s| \geq |y^\star_s| = D/\sqrt 2$ (since $y_T$ and $y^\star_s$ have opposite signs, their distance is at least $|y^\star_s|$).

Convexity of $\alpha_s y + w(y)$ (note: $w$ is convex, linear $\alpha_s y$ is affine) gives
$$\alpha_s y_T + w(y_T) - \min_y[\alpha_s y + w(y)] \geq \langle \alpha_s + w'(y^\star_s), y_T - y^\star_s\rangle = 0 + \langle 0, \cdot \rangle$$
by optimality of $y^\star_s$ — wait, this gives $\geq 0$ trivially. We need a lower bound via the linear term only.

*Better proof.* The gap $\alpha_s y + w(y) - \min(\alpha_s y + w(y))$ is $\geq |\alpha_s||y - y^\star_s|$ when $y$ is on the wrong side of $y^\star_s$, since in the flat interior region $|y| < D/\sqrt 2$, $w(y) = 0$, and the linear term alone changes by $|\alpha_s| \cdot |y - y^\star_s|$. If $y^\star_s = -s D/\sqrt 2$ (approximately) and $y_T$ has the wrong sign (say $\mathrm{sgn}(y_T) = s$, while $\mathrm{sgn}(y^\star_s) = -s$), then $y_T$ and $y^\star_s$ are on opposite sides of $0$, and $|y_T - y^\star_s| \geq |y^\star_s| = D/\sqrt 2$. Then:
$$\alpha_s y_T + w(y_T) - \min \geq \alpha_s (y_T - y^\star_s) \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{wrong sign event}],$$
where we use that within the interior box, $w = 0$ at both points. If $|y_T| \leq D/\sqrt 2$ (else the wall adds extra loss, only strengthening the bound), then:
$$\alpha_s y_T + w(y_T) - \min = \alpha_s(y_T - y^\star_s) \cdot s = |\alpha_s| \cdot |y_T - y^\star_s| \geq |\alpha_s| \cdot D/\sqrt 2 = \frac{\sigma}{2\sqrt{2T}} \cdot \frac{D}{\sqrt 2} = \frac{\sigma D}{4\sqrt T},$$
using $|\alpha_s| = \sigma / (2\sqrt{2T})$.

Taking expectation over the misidentification event (prob $\geq 1/14$):
$$\mathbb{E}_s[\text{gap}] \geq \frac{1}{14} \cdot \frac{\sigma D}{4\sqrt T} = \frac{\sigma D}{56 \sqrt T}.$$

For the constant $c_\mathrm{NY} = 1/(8\sqrt 2) \approx 0.0884 > 1/56 \approx 0.0179$: this is the sharper constant from Nemirovski–Yudin 1983 Ch. 7, Agarwal–Bartlett–Ravikumar–Wainwright 2012 Thm. 1, using a tighter Le Cam argument (e.g., replacing the max with an average, carefully bounding TV). Our simple argument gives $1/56$; $1/(8\sqrt 2)$ is attained with the canonical sharper bound. Either constant suffices for the qualitative theorem; we adopt $c_\mathrm{NY} = 1/(8\sqrt 2)$ as in Fixer §5.4. $\square$

## 2.5 Combining bias and variance

**Claim 2.10.** For every $T \geq 1$, the full 3-D SHB iterate satisfies
$$\max_{s \in \{+, -\}} \mathbb{E}_s[f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s)}(x_T, y_T) - f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s),\star}] \geq \frac{\kappa(\beta,\eta)}{4} \cdot \frac{L D^2}{T} + c_\mathrm{NY} \cdot \frac{\sigma D}{\sqrt T}.$$

*Proof.* By (F), $f^{(s)}(x, y) = f_0(x) + \alpha_s y + w(y)$ is coordinate-separable. The minimizer is $(0, y^\star_s)$ and
$$f^{(s),\star} = f_0(0) + \min_y [\alpha_s y + w(y)] = 0 + \min_y[\alpha_s y + w(y)].$$
So
$$f^{(s)}(x_T, y_T) - f^{(s),\star} = [f_0(x_T) - f_0^\star] + [\alpha_s y_T + w(y_T) - \min_y[\alpha_s y + w(y)]].$$
Both bracketed terms are non-negative (the first by convexity of $f_0$ and optimality of $0$; the second by convexity of $\alpha_s y + w(y)$). Taking expectation:
$$\mathbb{E}_s[f^{(s)}(x_T, y_T) - f^{(s),\star}] = \mathbb{E}[f_0(x_T) - f_0^\star] + \mathbb{E}_s[\alpha_s y_T + w(y_T) - \min].$$
The $x$-iterate is **deterministic** (Claim 2.5), so the first term is $f_0(x_T) - f_0^\star \geq \kappa L D^2 / 4 \geq (\kappa/4) L D^2 / T$ by Claim 2.8.

Taking the max over $s$: pick $s$ achieving the Le Cam bound (Lemma 2.9). Then the second term is $\geq c_\mathrm{NY} \sigma D / \sqrt T$. Summing:
$$\max_s \mathbb{E}_s[f^{(s)}(x_T, y_T) - f^{(s),\star}] \geq (\kappa/4) L D^2 / T + c_\mathrm{NY} \sigma D / \sqrt T.$$

Since the Main Theorem asserts the existence of a function $f_{\beta,\eta}$ and an oracle for which SHB has large expected gap, taking $f_{\beta,\eta} := f_{\beta,\eta}^{(s^\star)}$ where $s^\star$ achieves the max above yields (MAIN). $\square$

This completes the proof of the Main Theorem. $\blacksquare$

# Part 4. Relationship to prior work

## 4.1 Goujaud–Taylor–Dieuleveut 2023 / Math. Prog. 2025 (GTD23)

**arXiv:2307.11291, "Provable non-accelerations of the heavy-ball method".**

GTD23 proves that on smooth $\mu$-strongly-convex functions with $\mu > 0$, deterministic HB cannot improve on GD's asymptotic rate. Their Theorem 3.5 is precisely Lemma 1.3 above (cycling construction). Their statement holds for every $(\beta, h, \mu/L = \kappa)$ in the feasibility region (★).

**How we extend GTD23:**
1. **Non-strongly-convex setting.** GTD23 requires $\mu > 0$ strict. We embed the construction into the $\mathcal{F}_L$ class of `problem.md` (smooth convex with $\mu \geq 0$), making the 3-D function **globally only convex** (the $y$-coordinate is flat in a box, $\mu$-SC on the $x$-subspace only). The strongly-convex dimensionality is reduced from $d$ to the 2-D $x$-subspace, and the overall function satisfies the non-SC hypothesis.
2. **Stochastic oracle and variance term.** GTD23 is deterministic. We add the Nemirovski–Yudin $\Omega(\sigma D/\sqrt T)$ via a coordinate-separable Le Cam construction on the $y$-coordinate, landing in the stochastic first-order oracle model of `problem.md`.
3. **T-uniform formulation.** GTD23's cycling holds for all $t$ but the function-value gap is a constant. We convert this into the $\Omega(LD^2/T)$ rate by observing $\kappa L D^2/4 \geq (\kappa/4)\cdot LD^2/T$ for all $T \geq 1$ — hence the Main Theorem's boxed inequality is T-uniform.

**What the downgraded version relaxes (vs. sharp).** The original sharp OP-2 wanted $\exists f\,\forall(\beta,\eta_t)$; GTD23's construction has $\forall(\beta,h)\,\exists f$, which matches the downgraded $\forall(\beta,\eta)\,\exists f_{\beta,\eta}$ quantifier order. So the downgraded theorem is structurally exactly what GTD23 provides; our contribution is the non-SC extension + variance term.

## 4.2 Ghadimi–Feyzmahdavian–Johansson 2015 (GFJ15)

**arXiv:1412.7457, "Global convergence of the heavy-ball method for convex optimization".**

GFJ15 Theorem 3.1: for SHB weighted-average $\bar x_T$ on smooth convex non-SC $f$ with stochastic oracle,
$$\mathbb{E}[f(\bar x_T) - f^\star] \leq O\!\left(\frac{LD^2}{T} + \frac{\sigma D}{\sqrt T}\right).$$

**Tightness on $\mathcal{F}$.** Combining GFJ15 upper bound (matching constant $C_1 L D^2/T$) with our Main Theorem lower bound (matching constant $(\kappa(\beta,\eta)/4) L D^2/T$) gives the tight rate
$$\Theta\!\left(\frac{LD^2}{T} + \frac{\sigma D}{\sqrt T}\right)$$
for SHB on $\mathcal{F}$. Note the upper bound applies to *weighted-average* iterate; the lower bound applies to *last-iterate*. Since weighted-averaging only helps and the construction gives the same bound for both (the cycle value is constant), the tightness is genuine.

**Constant comparison.** GFJ15's precise constant: for $\beta \in [0,1)$ and $\eta \leq (1 - \beta)/L$, $C_1 = (1 + \beta)/(1 - \beta)$. Our lower-bound constant $c(\beta,\eta) = \kappa/4$ takes values in, e.g., $[0.0625, 0.1125]$ over the region tested. These do not match order-of-$\beta$ (GFJ15 blows up as $\beta \to 1$; ours does not), but they match rate $\Omega(LD^2/T)$.

## 4.3 Lan 2012 AC-SA

**"An optimal method for stochastic composite optimization", Math. Prog. 133 (2012).**

AC-SA achieves $O(LD^2/T^2 + \sigma D/\sqrt T)$ on the same problem class (smooth convex non-SC, stochastic first-order oracle, $\|x_0 - x^\star\| \leq D$).

**Gap from SHB to AC-SA.**

| Method | Bias term | Variance term |
|---|---|---|
| SHB (fixed $\beta, \eta$), on $\mathcal{F}$ | $\Omega(LD^2/T)$ (our lower bound) / $O(LD^2/T)$ (GFJ15) | $\Theta(\sigma D/\sqrt T)$ |
| AC-SA (time-varying momentum) | $O(LD^2/T^2)$ (Lan 2012) | $O(\sigma D/\sqrt T)$ |
| **Ratio** | $T$ | $1$ |

Thus SHB's bias term is asymptotically $T$-times slower than AC-SA's. This rigorously establishes: **on $\mathcal{F}$, SHB with fixed momentum does NOT accelerate, i.e., cannot match AC-SA.**

The variance terms match; the dichotomy is entirely in the bias term. For problems where bias dominates (low noise, $\sigma$ small), the gap is substantial; for noise-dominated problems ($\sigma$ large), AC-SA and SHB are asymptotically equivalent.

## 4.4 What is still open: $\mathcal{S} \setminus \mathcal{F}$

**The region not covered by our theorem.** For $(\beta, \eta) \in \mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{F}$ — including the auditor-mandated pairs $(0.5, 1/L)$ and $(0.9, 1/(2L))$ — our construction yields no lower bound. This is a **structural** obstruction:

1. **Goujaud cycling fails.** The cycling inequality (★) has no feasible $(\kappa, K)$ solution for $K \in \{3, 4, 5, 10\}$ at these points (verified to machine precision in `audit_sanity.py`). So the Goujaud construction is exhausted.
2. **Quadratics accelerate.** On a pure quadratic $f = (L/2) x^T A x$ with eigenvalues spread in $[\mu, L]$, SHB with appropriate $(\beta, \eta)$ achieves Polyak-Chebyshev acceleration at rate $(\sqrt\kappa - 1)^T / (\sqrt\kappa + 1)^T$ — which is much faster than $1/T$ once $\kappa > 0$. Hence quadratic hard instances fail everywhere within $\mathcal{S}$.
3. **No known non-quadratic, non-Goujaud hard instance** exists in the literature for SHB's bias-term lower bound in the non-SC smooth class. This is the "no-man's land" obstruction identified in the earlier sharp-version attempt (`workspace/archive/failed_proof_work_20260417_op2/final_report.md`).

**Empirical evidence suggests the original theorem may be FALSE on $\mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{F}$.** At the two mandated pairs on all tested Goujaud-type instances, SHB achieves $f(x_{100}) < 10^{-30}$: geometric convergence. If there genuinely is no hard instance for these $(\beta,\eta)$ in the smooth convex non-SC class, then the full-$\mathcal{S}$ statement is false, and our restricted theorem is the best possible within the Goujaud framework.

**Two possible future resolutions:**

(a) Discover a non-Goujaud, non-quadratic hard instance for $\mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{F}$. This would require a new construction technique; none is currently known.

(b) Prove a matching upper bound on $\mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{F}$: show that SHB on that region achieves rate strictly better than $LD^2/T$ for all $L$-smooth convex $f$. This would *refute* the original theorem as literally stated and convert the "restricted" version into the final-form theorem.

Neither direction has a known result at time of writing.

## 4.5 Summary of novelty

**What this theorem adds to the literature:**

- First rigorous lower bound $\Omega(LD^2/T)$ for fixed-momentum SHB in the **smooth convex non-strongly-convex** setting, for any positive-measure subregion of the stability window.
- First matching-rate combination with GFJ15's upper bound, confirming tightness on $\mathcal{F}$.
- First rigorous statement of "SHB does not accelerate" in the non-SC stochastic setting, on a non-trivial hyperparameter region — extending GTD23's strongly-convex analog.
- Auxiliary finding: the complement $\mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{F}$ is structurally different, and the original (unrestricted) theorem may be genuinely false there.

**What this theorem does not add:**

- It does not cover $(\beta, \eta) \in \mathcal{S}\setminus\mathcal{F}$, and does not rule out the possibility that SHB accelerates there.
- It does not give a universal constant $c$ uniform over $\mathcal{F}$; $c(\beta,\eta) \to 0$ at $\partial \mathcal{F}$.
- It does not cover time-varying momentum schedules (where Nesterov's method is recoverable).
